Anonymous trust assertions for Nostr

Trust scores you can verify without seeing who vouched.

nostr-veil is a privacy extension for NIP-85. A group vouches for someone, together; anyone can check the result; nobody can tell which members took part. Ring-signature privacy for Nostr reputation, built for the people who need trust systems most and can least afford to be named.

The problem

Reputation that exposes its contributors will never be used where it matters.

Nostr already has a standard for publishing trust scores: NIP-85, the Trusted Assertions spec. A provider publishes a score about someone, and you either trust the provider or you don't. But the scores are fully transparent. Anyone who can see a score can see exactly who gave it.

That is fine for benign signals like follower counts. It fails completely the moment the subject is sensitive. Whistleblowers, journalists, abuse reporters, political dissidents: the people who need reputation systems most are the ones who cannot afford to be identified.

The reason web-of-trust has never worked for sensitive contexts is not missing cryptography. It is that nobody built the layer that makes taking part safe. nostr-veil is that layer.

The trust trilemma

Verifiable. Private. Portable.

Standard NIP-85 makes you pick two. nostr-veil is the layer that delivers all three.

Verifiable

NIP-85 yes  /  veil yes

Every score is backed by signatures anyone can check. The maths, not a brand, is what you trust.

Private

NIP-85 no  /  veil yes

Standard NIP-85 reveals exactly who gave every score. veil hides each contributor inside the circle. This is the property nothing else delivers.

Portable

NIP-85 yes  /  veil yes

The output is an ordinary NIP-85 event on ordinary relays. Any Nostr client reads it, today, unchanged.

How it works

Four steps, no names.

01

Form a trust circle

A fixed group of public keys agrees to assess a subject together. The membership list is public; who signs is not.

02

Contribute anonymously

Each member's score is wrapped in an LSAG ring signature: proof that some member of the circle signed, with no way to tell which. A duplicate-detection token stops anyone signing twice.

03

Aggregate into a standard event

Contributions combine, median by default, into an ordinary NIP-85 event. Every Nostr client reads it as a normal trust score.

04

Anyone verifies the proof

A veil-aware client checks every ring signature and the threshold. It learns that K distinct members agreed. It never learns who they were.

// the whole flow, four calls
import { createTrustCircle, contributeAssertion,
         aggregateContributions, verifyProof } from 'nostr-veil'

const circle = createTrustCircle([alice, bob, carol])

const a = contributeAssertion(circle, subject, { rank: 74 }, aliceKey, 0)
const b = contributeAssertion(circle, subject, { rank: 80 }, bobKey,   1)

const assertion = aggregateContributions(circle, subject, [a, b])
const result    = verifyProof(assertion)
// { valid: true, circleSize: 3, threshold: 2, distinctSigners: 2 }
Why you can trust it

Cryptography you can build on.

A proven primitive

Ring signatures are not new. Monero has used them in production since 2014. nostr-veil applies that cryptography; it does not invent it.

The curve Nostr already uses

Signatures are built on secp256k1, the same elliptic curve as Bitcoin and every Nostr key. No new cryptographic trust assumptions.

Independently maintained

The ring-signature engine is its own published, separately tested npm package, on its own release cycle and useful well beyond nostr-veil.

What it unlocks

Seven things you can build on it today.

Every one of these runs on shipped code. None of them is possible with reputation that names its contributors.

Moderation

Anonymous abuse reporting

A moderation circle flags a serial harasser. The platform sees that five distinct moderators agreed, enough to act on, but no moderator's name is attached. None of them can be singled out for reprisal.

Journalism

Source corroboration

Several contacts who have dealt with a would-be source independently vouch that they are who they claim. An editor confirms the threshold was met. The list of who vouched is never written down, so it cannot be subpoenaed.

Research

Anonymous peer review

A circle of reviewers scores a paper or a researcher. The verdict is verifiable and it stands. A junior reviewer can rate a powerful figure honestly without their next grant depending on it.

Onboarding

Sybil-resistant vouching

Established members co-sign that a new account is a real person. The newcomer carries a portable proof that enough members vouched, without anyone publishing a follow graph that maps the whole community.

Communities

Federated moderation

Moderation circles across several communities assess the same bad actor. A shared scope lets a verifier count the distinct moderators who flagged them across all of them. Someone in three circles counts once, not three times, still with no names.

Fact-checking

Claim and content verification

A circle of fact-checkers scores a specific post. Readers see a verifiable "nine of twelve checkers rated this accurate" on the note itself. A checker who contradicts a popular claim is not left exposed to the pile-on.

Commerce

Real-world trade reputation

Past counterparties of a trader rate how the deal actually went. A new counterparty sees a verifiable track record. The people who left honest, critical marks cannot be identified and retaliated against.

On the roadmap

What is shipped, and what is next.

Shipped today is the core: anonymous circles, threshold aggregation, signed-metric verification, and cross-circle federation. Next, honestly labelled:

planned Revocation. A contributor can withdraw a vouch they no longer stand behind, verifiably, without revealing who they are.
planned Time-bounded trust. Assertions that carry an expiry, so a score reflects now and not five years ago.
planned Anonymous endorsements. A circle co-signs a credential or attestation, not only a numeric score.
planned Anonymous gated access. A trust circle doubles as an anonymous membership group, so a relay or community can admit members without learning which member just arrived.
